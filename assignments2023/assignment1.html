# Basic probability theory notation and terms

This can be trivial or you may need to refresh your memory on these concepts (see, e.g. Aalto course *First Course in Probability and
Statistics*). Explain each of the following terms with one sentence:

-   probability
-   probability mass (function)
-   probability density (function)
-   probability distribution
-   discrete probability distribution
-   continuous probability distribution
-   cumulative distribution function (cdf)
-   likelihood

::: {.content-hidden when-profile="public"}

The correct answers are roughly the following:

- probability: a measure between 0 and 1 of a likelihood that an event will occur.
- probability mass: a value between 0 and 1 that tells what is a probability of some discrete event.
- probability density: a function that outputs relative probabilities for given regions in the domain of a random variable
- probability distribution: a function that gives probabilities of outcomes of different random variables.
- discrete probability distribution: a probability distribution of discrete values.
- continuous probability distribution: a probability distribution of values that come from a
continuous space
- cumulative distribution function: a function that gives the probability that a certain random value
will be less or equal than the real valued input
- likelihood: p(y|theta) as a function of theta, epistemic information obtained from the data through the
likelihood, proportional to the probability distribution (not just the probability, because it can be also
density) of theta given uniform prior. Sometimes used also to refer to the observation model/statistical
model/sampling distribution.

Here are some probability values that can help you figure
out if you copied the right conditional probabilities from the
question.

-   P(Test gives positive \| Subject does not have lung cancer) =
    $4\%$
-   P(Test gives positive **and** Subject has lung cancer) =
    $0.098\%$ this is also referred to as the **joint probability**
    of *test being positive* and the *subject having lung cancer*.

::: {.content-hidden when-profile="public"}

* $p(\theta|y) = \frac{p(\theta) p(y|\theta)}{p(y)}$: Bayes' rule
* $\theta=1$: subject has cancer
* $y=1$: subject tests positive
* $p(\theta=1) \approx \frac{1}{1000} = 0.1\%$: prior probability of having cancer
* $p(\theta=0) = 1 - p(\theta=1) \approx 99.9\%$: prior probability of not having cancer
* $p(y=1|\theta=1) = 98\%$: probability to test positive if one has cancer
* $p(y=0|\theta=0) = 96\%$: probability to test negative if one does not have cancer
* $p(y=1|\theta=0) = 1 - p(y=0|\theta=0) = 4\%$: probability to test positive if one does not have cancer
* $p(y=1) = p(y=1|\theta=0)p(\theta=0) + p(y=1|\theta=1)p(\theta=1) = 4\% \cdot 99.9\%+98\% \cdot 0.1\% = 4.094\%$: prior probability of testing positive
* $p(\theta=1|y=1) = \frac{p(\theta=1) p(y=1|\theta=1)}{p(y=1)} \approx \frac{0.1\% \cdot 98\%}{4.094\%} \approx 2.39\%$: **probability of having cancer after testing positive**

:::

function p_identical_twin(fraternal_prob=1/150, identical_prob=1/400)
    twin_probabilities = [fraternal_prob, identical_prob]
    brother_probabilities = [.5, 1.]
    # Is odds the right word?
    posterior_odds = twin_probabilities .* brother_probabilities
    return posterior_odds[2] / sum(posterior_odds)
end
